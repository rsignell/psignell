## Part 1 — Family, Childhood & Education

**Video:** [youtu.be/atwNRg4E1mU](https://youtu.be/atwNRg4E1mU)

### Summary

The interview opens with Peter explaining the origin of his surname. His grandfather emigrated from Sweden in his early twenties — likely to avoid military conscription — and settled in Rockford, Illinois, where a large Swedish immigrant community lived. The Swedish king had decreed that families must choose hereditary last names (previously patronymic), and his grandfather chose "Signell." There is reportedly only one other Peter Signell in the world: a Swedish business consultant and former head of the Swedish Buddhists.

Peter was born in 1928, a year before the Great Depression. His father was a civil engineer who worked on the Lake of the Ozarks dam, then returned to graduate school before being hired to lead **CCC (Civilian Conservation Corps)** camps, helping farmers with conservation and flood control. This led to the family moving nearly every year — Wisconsin, Iowa, Milwaukee, and eventually Washington, D.C. — as his father rose to Assistant Director of the Soil Conservation Service.

In Milwaukee, Peter had a newspaper delivery route stretching miles into the wealthy suburb of River Hills, riding through freezing winter conditions. He describes it as "a little like torture" but takes pride in having completed it.

High school in Washington, D.C. (Western High School) brought two formative experiences:

1. **ROTC/Band** — Rather than infantry, he joined the school band, where he befriended Paul Gardeski (voted "most likely to succeed"), who introduced him to folk dancing at a grand mansion off Dupont Circle.
2. **Dr. McPherson** — A tiny 70-year-old English teacher with extraordinary command of her classroom. When Peter summarized Gogol's *The Overcoat* superficially, she stopped him: *"That is not what it is about. It is about the condition of mankind."* He calls it "one of the great moments of my life."

For college, his father directed him to **Antioch College** in Yellow Springs, Ohio — an unconventional school founded on a work-study model (alternating semesters at college with co-op jobs in the wider world). Peter arrived in his ROTC dress uniform, unaware Antioch was deeply anti-military.

His most significant co-op job was in **Alaska** (early 1950s, Korean War era), where he and three friends drove a $220 car north, draining the radiator into milk jugs each night to avoid buying antifreeze. He ended up as a field engineer on a remote island, responsible for building placement for 550 construction workers. The Secretary of War personally visited to urge them to finish the job, given uncertainty about Soviet intentions toward Alaska. His draft board tried to conscript him; the commanding general in Alaska overruled them.

Returning to Antioch, his outlook changed. He pursued a woman named **Liz** — who would become his wife — after she agreed to help gather firewood for the Morgan House fireplace on a snowy evening.

The head of Antioch's science division told him he wasn't suited to engineering but had the makings of a theoretical physicist. A pivotal scene: the department chairman stormed into his professor's office waving Peter's "dreadful" exam. The professor replied: *"You're an experimentalist. He's a theorist."* Peter thought: *"Theorist? What's that? I'm a theorist."*

He went to the **University of Rochester** for his PhD, working under **Robert Marshak**, one of the leading figures in high-energy physics. He taught himself quantum mechanics from Dirac's notoriously difficult textbook over a summer and found it "thrilling." He defended his thesis, married Liz on graduation day (she walked between rows of chairs on the front lawn; he appeared from behind a tree), and they honeymooned by canoe in the Boundary Waters wilderness. En route, he fixed a blown head gasket roadside — impressing his new bride.

He later worked a co-op job in the home laboratory of **Charles Kettering**, Vice President for Research at General Motors and inventor of the motorized cash register and co-founder of Delco. Despite immense wealth, Kettering drove a plain black Chevrolet while his wife had a custom-built olive-colored Cadillac. Peter took teaching positions at Bucknell University and Penn State before moving to Michigan State.

### Summary

Part 2 picks up at Michigan State, where Peter moved after a "crazy dean" at Penn State insisted on attaching himself to all grants, driving about half the physics department to leave.

A cardiologist told Peter he was a "Type A" heart attack risk and should become a Type B personality. He resolved to change overnight. His conversion was later confirmed when, arriving a few minutes late to an international conference at Stony Brook, the chairman spotted him from the stage and shouted across the packed auditorium: *"What happened to you, Signell? Somebody pull your teeth?"* Peter smiled, waved, and sat down in the back — knowing he had genuinely changed.

Seeking an unoccupied niche, Peter pivoted to **physics education development and research**. After an initial small grant from the Carnegie Corporation, he wrote a large proposal for the **National Science Foundation** — funded at a higher level than requested. He became the lead-off speaker at an NSF project directors' meeting in Washington; the auditorium displayed a thermometer chart of grant sizes with his at the top. He gave talks around the country, but found that most people who invited him simply wanted him to publish their rejected textbooks.

His teaching system was innovative:
- Self-paced written lessons, each with explicit prerequisite and output skills
- Untimed exams requiring research-paper-style write-ups (correct reasoning counted; the answer alone did not)
- Optional lectures, which students eventually stopped attending entirely
- Upper-division engineering students serving as graders and consultants
- Elevator pitches and demonstrations as assessment tools

At peak enrollment, he had **over 1,000 students** across roughly 14 courses — with no significant student complaints. The main friction came from administrators who feared the model implied fewer faculty were needed. He retired at 73 when the department handed his course system to a younger faculty member.

His wife **Elizabeth** developed kyphoscoliosis, becoming severely bent over. Her lungs failed and she died of pneumonia; a pulmonologist saw her for the first time the day before she died. Peter and their three sons were all present at her death.

He now companions **Joan** — a longtime friend of Elizabeth's whose husband had also died — going to symphony concerts, Sunday Unitarian church forums, lunching on her patio, and combining grocery trips (she provides a Tuesday supper; he provides the car). They live in their own homes but are effectively family: Peter appears in Joan's family portraits, and she in his.

He reflects on raising **Richard, Steve, and Eric** — born with five- and ten-year gaps — so "there was never sibling rivalry; each child was the only child when they were little." The family camped across the American West, visited Florida and Hawaii, and spent a year in Bonn, Germany when Peter was a guest professor. His grandson **Alex** began at Stony Brook University and told Peter he would of course study theoretical physics.

### Summary

Part 3 opens with Peter discussing his religious life. During a co-op job in Phoenix he sampled many denominations — including Baha'i — and settled on the **Unitarian Church**. He and Joan attend a weekly Sunday forum of roughly 20 people who discuss current civic and global issues, prepare readings in advance, and contribute links to relevant material. During the pandemic they met via Zoom, which kept them in meaningful contact.

Asked how he afforded college, Peter explains he **graduated debt-free**. His parents contributed only about $700–800 total over six years. The Alaska co-op job — working nearly around the clock for an extended season — paid him a clear $250/week, exceptional for the era, and he saved enough to cover the rest. He also won a Johnson Wax Company scholarship by articulating, in Antioch's tradition (invoking Horace Mann's founding charge: *"Be ashamed to die until you have done something for humanity"*), what he intended to do with his education.

His three sons' college costs: **Richard** attended the University of Michigan (in-state rate) and then MIT, which gave him a full ride plus a stipend — costing Peter nothing. **Steve** also attended U of M. **Eric** attended Michigan State, where Peter was faculty.

Peter describes how Michigan State's physics department — tired of administrative overreach — took advantage of a trustee window to write and pass **self-governing bylaws**, including the right to elect their own chairman and establish an elected faculty salary review committee. His own pay suffered because he stopped publishing in high-energy physics as he moved into education research and didn't bother submitting annual self-evaluations, but he says: *"That's okay. We did fine."*

The final extended section covers the **history of Heron Island**, a small island in Maine where the Signell family has long had cottages. Peter was the island's webmaster for about ten years (the site was at javis.org) and researched roughly 17 historical documents from the island's early days.

Key island history:
- Someone once cleared the north shore woods to graze sheep; it proved unprofitable.
- Around 1885, a group of Boston investors bought the island, divided it into hundreds of tiny lots, and built a grand hotel. Liz's relative managed that hotel around the turn of the 20th century, establishing the family's connection to the island.
- **Levi Gunn** and Ed Damon were the last officers of the Heron Island Company; Gunn charged residents for docking, water, and anything else he could, and was widely resented.
- When Gunn died, his widow — entirely unlike him — hired lawyers to help residents form **JAVIS** (the Heron Island Homeowners Association). Josh Thompson, Gunn's social enemy in the cottage by the tennis court, refused to join for decades; his daughter Mary Thompson finally joined years later, to great applause.
- The hotel burned down in 1918, widely believed to be an insurance fire — no newspaper of the day mentioned it, and no one talked about it.
- The island's tennis cup, won three times in a row by Liz's cousin Bill in the 1920s–30s, sits on the mantle at the Barnacle.

Peter now owns a share of the **Barnacle** cottage jointly with his son Steve (Steve manages it and will eventually inherit Peter's share); Rich owns **Morris Cottage**, which Peter gave him. Eric, the western son, seldom visits — his partner finds the island too hot and humid.

The interview closes warmly, with the young interviewer planning to visit Heron Island before leaving for college, and Peter inviting him to come.
